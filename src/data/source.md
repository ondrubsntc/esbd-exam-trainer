## Q1 — Marketing and its role in a small company; essential marketing tools; strategic and operational level

### THEORY

**What marketing is** Marketing is everything a company does to find what customers need, create a product for it, set a price, tell people about it, and get it to them — in a way that makes money. Selling is only one part of marketing. Marketing starts *before* the product exists and keeps going *after* the sale.

**What marketing does in a small company** Three main jobs:

- **Understand the market** — who the customer is, what they want, who the competitors are.
- **Find opportunities** — spot needs nobody is meeting yet.
- **Support sales and build value** — get the product to the right customer with a clear reason to buy.

In a small firm there’s little money, so marketing has to be focused and measurable, not mass-market. It’s usually run by the owner.

**Essential marketing tools — the marketing mix** The set of tools a company mixes together to reach its goals.

- **4P (seller’s view):** *Product* (quality, design, brand, packaging) · *Price* (price, discounts, payment terms) · *Place* (where and how it’s sold) · *Promotion* (ads, sales promotion, PR, direct marketing, personal selling)
- **5P** = 4P + *People*
- **7P (services)** = 5P + *Process* (how it’s sold/delivered) + *Physical evidence* (the place the customer sees it — shop, showroom, e-shop)
- **4C (customer’s view):** *Customer solution* (what problem it solves) · *Customer cost* (total cost, not just price) · *Convenience* (how easy it is to get) · *Communication* (talking *with* the customer, not *at* them)

**Strategic vs. operational level**

- **Strategic = long-term, big picture.** Which markets to go after, how to stand out, how to position the brand. Answers *where do we compete and why.*
- **Operational = short-term, daily work.** The actual campaigns and channels, using people and resources efficiently. Answers *what do we do this week.*

Operational work is how the strategy gets done — the two must match.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

- **For us, marketing *was* the problem.** Good service, but no way to reach clients — they only came by referral, so growth couldn’t be planned. That’s exactly what marketing is for: connecting a good service to the right people.
- **Understanding the market:** I worked in Dubai as an agent first, so I knew it from the inside. I also interviewed 17 clients/prospects and mystery-shopped a competitor.
- **Marketing mix — we’re a service, so 7P:** *Product* = advice + hand-picked properties · *Price* = commission paid by the developer · *Place* = remote/cross-border · *Promotion* = Meta Ads, personal brand, landing page · *People* = me + external video/marketing/web team · *Process* = the 8-step funnel · *Physical evidence* = landing page + personal brand (trust is everything in a big foreign deal).
- **Strategic vs. operational** is how the whole thesis is split: *strategic* = position myself as a trustworthy advisor, pick the target group (Czech/Slovak investors), build trust; *operational* = run the ads, landing page, retargeting, lead qualification (Calendly + Airtable).
- **4C in real life:** clients aren’t buying “a service” — they’re buying *a safe way into a confusing foreign market without getting burned*, and the brand runs on *communication* (two-way trust), not loud promotion.

*(Contrast: in dropshipping and the German e-shop, marketing was mostly operational — FB + IG ads for quick B2C sales. Dubai is the opposite: the strategic, trust-building side matters most.)*

## Q2 — Marketing analysis: general environment, industry, and internal factors (tools, methods, outputs)

### THEORY

Marketing analysis looks at the company from two sides: the **internal environment** (microenvironment — inside the company) and the **external environment** (macroenvironment — outside the company). The findings from both are then pulled together into a **SWOT**.

**Internal analysis (microenvironment)** Looks at the company’s own situation and position. Tools:

- **Marketing mix analysis (4P/7P)** — checking product, price, place, promotion (+ people, process, physical evidence for services).
- **Communication mix analysis** — all the tools the company uses to communicate (advertising, PR, personal selling, direct marketing, sponsoring) and whether they fit together.
- **7S** — another internal model.

The microenvironment is shaped by the players close to the company: customers, suppliers, distributors, competitors, and the public.

**External analysis (macroenvironment)** Looks at outside forces the company mostly *can’t* control. Main tools:

- **SLEPT analysis** (and variants like PESTLE) — outside factors:
- *Social* — population, age, culture, behaviour
- *Legislative/Political* — laws, regulations, government conditions
- *Economic* — purchasing power, wages, GDP, spending structure
- *Technological* — level of technology, machinery, innovation
- *Ecological* — sustainability, renewable resources (the “E” in PESTLE)
- **Porter’s Five Forces** — competitive pressure in the industry:
- *New entrants* — threat of new competitors
- *Existing competitors* — rivalry in the market
- *Substitutes* — threat of being replaced
- *Suppliers* — their bargaining power
- *Customers/buyers* — their bargaining power
- **Customer analysis — RFM** (used to segment customers):
- *Recency* — how recently they bought
- *Frequency* — how often they buy
- *Monetary* — how much they spend RFM helps with targeting, campaign settings, and lowering marketing cost.

**Output — SWOT** All findings are summarized into a SWOT:

- *Strengths / Weaknesses* = **internal** factors the company can control.
- *Opportunities / Threats* = **external** factors it can’t control.

**Industrial (B2B) market specifics:** higher turnover than consumer markets; demand depends on demand in the consumer market; customized production; low price flexibility; the buyer is an organization (team decision); customers are geographically concentrated, so suppliers move closer to them.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

- **External (SLEPT) was central:** the *Political/Legal* factor was so strong that I paused paid campaigns because of Middle-East geopolitical risk. *Economic* = the purchasing power of Czech/Slovak investors and whether property abroad looks attractive vs. local options.
- **Porter’s forces:** *suppliers* = Dubai developers (I depend on their access and product); *substitutes* = other investment markets or other countries; *competitors* = other advisors selling Dubai property — which I studied directly through **mystery shopping**.
- **SWOT:** *Strength* = real inside-market knowledge and developer contacts; *Weakness* = acquisition depended on referrals; *Opportunity* = a clear gap for a trustworthy advisor brand; *Threat* = regional instability + aggressive competitors pushing bad developments.
- **Customer analysis:** the thesis did a deep target-audience analysis (psychographics, pains, desires) — the same idea as RFM-style customer profiling, used to target the right investor.

*(German e-shop: SLEPT economic factor = price gap between CZ and DE; Porter supplier power = my single German supplier.)*

## Q3 — Market segmentation: overview, tools, methods, outputs; B2C vs B2B

### THEORY

**What it is** Segmentation means splitting a market into smaller groups of customers who share similar characteristics and needs. It makes the company more efficient with time and money — you focus on the groups most likely to buy.

**Why it helps (outputs/advantages):**

- A **stronger marketing message** — easier to talk to people when you know who they are.
- Picking the **right tools and tactics** for that group.
- **Targeted ads** using the segment’s characteristics.
- **Brand differentiation** by focusing on specific needs.
- **Deeper relationships** — you can offer exactly what they want.
- Finding **niche opportunities** — underserved groups to build new products for.

**Four basic types of segmentation:**

- **Demographic** — age, gender, education, income, family situation
- **Psychographic** — lifestyle, hobbies, interests, values, attitudes
- **Geographic** — country, region, city size
- **Behavioral** — buying habits, spending habits, user status, brand interactions

A **segmentation model** is a tool (often built in a database) that scores and ranks customers against chosen criteria.

**B2C vs B2B segmentation**

- **B2C criteria:** geographic, demographic, psychographic, technographic (devices/tech used). B2C markets: value driven by customers, strong focus on brand, usually short-term buying, promotion aimed at individuals.
- **B2B criteria:** demographic (company size, industry), type of organization (production/services/trade), geographic, purchase situation (new vs. existing customer, power structure). B2B buying is a team decision and more rational.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

- **This is B2C, and psychographic segmentation was the key.** I didn’t target “everyone with money” — the thesis profiled the target investor by their *psychographics, pains, and desires* (people who want a safe entry into Dubai, fear getting scammed, want a trustworthy guide).
- **Demographic + geographic layer:** Czech/Slovak investors with enough capital to buy abroad.
- **Output = sharper message + targeted ads:** once I knew the segment, the Meta Ads and landing page could speak directly to *that* person’s fear and goal, instead of generic “buy property in Dubai.”

*(Dropshipping / German e-shop: classic B2C demographic + behavioral segmentation — targeting FB/IG audiences by age, interests, and buying behaviour.)*

## Q4 — Marketing communication: overview, tools, methods; traditional and digital channels

### THEORY

**What it is** Marketing communication is all the messaging — through every channel — that affects what customers know and how they behave toward the product. The goal: persuade the customer to act (to buy).

**Channels**

- **Traditional:** face-to-face, newspapers, TV, radio, direct mail, phone, flyers.
- **Digital / online (the biggest part today):** website, social media, email, live chat, SEO, PPC (pay-per-click), CTR (click-through rate). Online marketing ties into the other communication tools and builds brand awareness.

**Target groups**

- *Customers* — reached with the communication mix.
- *Non-customers* — reached with corporate communication (employees, media, government).

**AIDA model** (how a message moves a customer to buy):

- **A — Attention:** make them aware
- **I — Interest:** show who you are / the brand
- **D — Desire:** make them want it
- **A — Action:** the purchase

**CRM (Customer Relationship Management):** managing the long-term relationship to create loyal customers and maximize profit on both sides.

**Tools of the communication mix:**

- **Advertising** — paid, impersonal promotion; huge reach; builds awareness and image.
- **Sales promotion** — short-term motivators: discounts, free samples, gifts, competitions, loyalty cards.
- **Public relations (PR)** — building trust and a positive image; credibility-focused.
- **Direct marketing** — personal, customized contact (email, phone).
- **Event marketing** — events for personal interaction and emotional bonds.
- **Personal selling** — face-to-face persuasion; low reach but very high impact.
- **Sponsoring** — funding cultural/social/media activities to support the brand image.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

- **Channel mix:** I started purely **traditional** (word of mouth, networking — that’s how the first clients came). The thesis moves it to **digital**: Meta Ads, a personal brand on social media, a landing page, plus WhatsApp and email for nurturing.
- **AIDA is literally the funnel:** *Attention* = the Meta Ad stops the scroll; *Interest* = the educational landing page; *Desire* = personal-brand content + retargeting build trust and want; *Action* = booking a call (Calendly) and closing.
- **Communication mix tools used:** *advertising* (Meta Ads), *direct marketing* (WhatsApp/email nurturing), *personal selling* (the consultative sales call — low reach, very high impact, perfect for a high-value deal), *PR/personal brand* (sharing market insights to build credibility).
- **CRM:** lead and client data tracked in **Airtable** to manage the relationship over time.

*(Dropshipping / German e-shop: mostly advertising — FB + IG ads — plus sales promotion like discounts to push quick purchases.)*

## Q5 — Pricing: overview, tools, methods

### THEORY

**What it is** Pricing is setting the value (price) for a product or service. Price isn’t only about money — it also includes the customer’s non-money costs (time, effort, energy).

**Two groups of factors affecting price:**

- **Internal** — the company *can* control these: goals, profit targets, market-share targets.
- **External** — mostly *can’t* control: competition, demand, the economic situation.

**Pricing process (steps):**

- **Set the pricing goal** — maximize profit, turnover, market share, or just survive.
- **Analyze demand.**
- **Estimate costs** — *Fixed* (don’t change with volume) + *Variable* (rise with volume); *Average* = (FC + VC) / units.
- **Analyze competitors’ prices and offers.**
- **Set the final price.**

**Pricing methods:**

- **Demand pricing** — price based on what customers will pay at different demand levels. (*Elastic demand* = sales react strongly to price; *inelastic demand* = sales barely react.)
- **Competitive pricing** — price set close to competitors; common in oligopoly/commodity markets (steel, wood, paper).
- **Cost pricing** — the most common: calculate cost + add profit. *Cost-plus* = add a % markup to the unit cost to get the selling price.

**Pricing strategies:**

- **Price skimming** — start with a high price (new, special product), lower it later as competitors enter.
- **Market penetration pricing** — start low to enter fast and grab market share; needs high volume and a financially strong company.

**Discounts (“rewards” for customers):** quantity, seasonal, early-payment, buy-one-get-one.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

- **Unusual model — the client pays nothing directly.** My price is a **commission paid by the developer**, not by the client. So “pricing” toward the client is really about *value*, not a number — the customer cost is mostly their *time and risk*, which my service lowers.
- **Internal vs. external factors:** I can’t move the developer commission much (external), so I compete on *trust and value*, not price.

*(Strongest pricing examples are the e-commerce projects:)*

- **German e-shop:** classic **cost-plus** pricing — buy in Germany, add markup, but stay below the Czech price (the whole point is the CZ–DE price gap). Also **competitive pricing** against Czech sellers of the same goods.
- **Dropshipping:** tested **demand pricing** — try different prices on Shopify and watch which converts; used markups that still allowed profitable FB/IG ad spend.

## Q6 — Purchase management and sales management

### THEORY

### Purchasing

Buying the goods and services a company needs at the best cost from a reliable source. It includes negotiation, contracts, and the buying process. Purchasing matters in every company size — without buying, there’s no business, and purchase cost is the first thing that shapes product cost and profit.

**Objectives of purchasing:** safe, on-time, sufficient supply · the right quality · the lowest possible cost · innovation from suppliers · competitive advantage.

**Types of purchasing systems:**

- **Tender** — invite suppliers, lowest offer wins (open / restricted / negotiated). Used by big firms and government.
- **Stockless purchase** — supplier holds the stock and releases it when needed (just-in-time); supplier carries the financial risk of holding inventory.
- **Blanket order** — for many small everyday items that are hard to keep in inventory.
- **E-purchasing** — buying/selling online (B2B, B2C, B2G) to cut cost and raise efficiency.
- **Capital equipment purchase** — high-value assets (machinery).
- **Outsourcing** — handing some work to an outside firm (e.g. an external accountant).
- **Petty cash** — small cash fund for tiny purchases.

**Purchasing management functions (the buying process):** receive request → review/evaluate → select supplier (tender) → review offers → place order → market research/supplier development → authorize payment (after goods arrive).

**Purchase marketing mix** — tools for the buying side: information mix, communication mix, supplier mix, competitive mix, price mix, product mix, quality mix, quantity mix, term mix, mix of purchase conditions.

### Sales management

Planning, directing, and controlling personal selling — plus recruiting, training, leading, and paying salespeople — to hit the company’s sales goals efficiently.

**Roles of a sales manager:** strategic (forecasting, long-term plans), corporate team member (company goals), team leader (sales targets), managing sales channels, CRM.

**Needed skills:** *people* (motivating, leading, communicating), *managing* (planning, organizing, admin), *technical* (training, negotiating, CRM).

**Sales management levels:** strategic/top (president) · tactical/middle (regional managers) · operational/first line (branch sales manager).

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

**Purchasing — German e-shop (*best fit*):**

- **Supplier selection + outsourcing:** I chose an **external supplier in Germany** and outsourced fulfilment to him — he ships directly to customers. That’s **outsourcing/subcontracting** in practice.
- **Stockless / just-in-time:** I held **no inventory** — the supplier released goods only when a customer ordered. That’s textbook *stockless purchasing*, and it’s exactly the **dropshipping** model with Štěpán too.
- **Quality vs. price mix:** the supplier choice was driven by Germany’s better price/quality — the “supplier mix” and “quality mix” decisions in real life.

**Sales management — Ondruš & Partners (Dubai):**

- I act as the **sales manager and the salesperson** at once. The “personal selling” skills matter most because it’s one-to-one with a high-value client.
- **CRM + control:** I track leads and follow-ups in Airtable, forecast roughly how many leads turn into deals, and manage the pipeline — that’s the strategic/control side of sales management.

## Q7 — Sales and integrated marketing communication

### THEORY

**Sales management** (same base as Q6): planning, directing, and controlling personal selling to reach sales goals.

- **Field sales team** — contacts current and future customers.
- **Headquarters team** — supports the field team.

**Planning hierarchy:** objectives (measurable targets) → strategies (how to reach them) → tactics (the action plan).

**Sales management process:** Conception → Planning → Execution → Control → Feedback.

**Selling process (steps):**

- **Pre-sale preparation** — before the customer is acquired.
- **Prospecting & planning** — find and qualify leads (use loyal customers, social media); the *pre-approach* (planning just before contact) is one of the most important steps.
- **Communicating the sales message** — relationship-building, the sales presentation, a good first impression, focusing on the customer’s benefit.
- **Negotiation** — aim for a win-win.
- **Closing the sale + follow-up** — attitude matters; both sides should feel they won.

**CRM** — a business model to grow revenue by focusing on customers: customer *retention*, *acquisition*, and *profitability*. Advantages: lower ad costs, easier targeting, competing on service instead of price.

**Sales promotion** — motivational tools to push faster/bigger purchases. Types:

- **Direct marketing** — direct contact (email, phone, internet).
- **Direct sale** — personal presentation, two-way seller↔buyer.
- **Viral marketing** — low cost, customers spread the message.
- **Guerrilla marketing** — provocative, unconventional, low cost.
- **Product placement** — paid placement in content; *in-game advertising*.
- **POP/POS material** — point-of-purchase/sale, influences the customer right when deciding.
- **Cross promotion** — two products support each other (“buy both, get a discount”).

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

- **Consultative selling, not transactional:** because it’s a high-value, high-trust deal, I sell by *asking questions and advising* (Socratic method), not by pushing. This is the heart of the thesis’s sales approach.
- **The full selling process is the back half of my funnel:** *prospecting/qualification* (Meta Ads + Calendly + Airtable) → *sales message* (the strategy presentation) → *negotiation & nurturing* (WhatsApp) → *closing* (developer integration) → *follow-up* (keeping the relationship for referrals).
- **CRM in action:** retention and referrals are huge — happy clients sent me my first deals. Airtable tracks acquisition and follow-up.
- **Direct marketing:** WhatsApp and email nurturing are direct, one-to-one channels.

*(Dropshipping: used several sales-promotion types — discounts, urgency offers, and near-viral/scroll-stopping creative on FB/IG; POS-style nudges at checkout.)*

## Q8 — The economy using the AS–AD model (recession, overheating, potential output, growth, business cycles)

### THEORY

**Business cycle** — the up-and-down movement of GDP around its long-term growth trend. Regular pattern of expansion and contraction. **Four phases:** Peak → Recession → Trough → Recovery.

**AS–AD model:** shifting the **AD** (aggregate demand) or **AS** (aggregate supply) curve = a *shock*. Positive shock moves the curve right; negative shock moves it left.

- **Demand-side cycle:** if investment or consumer spending falls, AD shifts **left** → both output (Q) and price (P) fall. A sudden drop = a *demand shock*.
- **Supply-side cycle:** falling productivity or rising input prices (e.g. an oil/energy price jump) = a *supply shock* → AS shifts left → prices rise, output falls.

**Output gap** = the difference between *actual* output and *potential* output (what the economy could produce at full employment).

- If actual < potential → **recession** (output gap, higher unemployment).
- If actual > potential → **inflation gap** (prices rise).

**Key states:**

- **Recession** — a period of falling real output: shrinking production and consumption, higher unemployment, lower prices; visible as a drop in real GDP.
- **Overheated economy** — unemployment temporarily *below* the natural rate; output above potential. Always temporary; rising inflation; producers can’t keep up with demand.
- **Reaching potential output** — the highest real GDP that can be sustained long-term, at the natural rate of unemployment.
- **Long-term economic growth** — growth of *potential* output over time; measured as the % change in real GDP. Driven only by **supply-side factors**: natural resources, capital accumulation & technology (the main one), and market size.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

*(This is a macro topic — the link to projects is lighter, but here’s a real one I can speak to:)*

- **A real “shock” decision in Dubai:** the Middle-East **geopolitical situation** acted like a *negative shock* on my market — it raised uncertainty and would have cut demand for cross-border property investment. That’s exactly why I **paused the paid campaigns**: launching aggressive ads into a falling-confidence environment would have wasted money and broken my advisory positioning.
- **Real estate and the cycle:** property markets like Dubai’s move with the business cycle — booms (near a *peak*, prices and demand high) and corrections. My inside-market knowledge is partly about spotting which developments are overheated/overhyped vs. genuinely sound.
- **Long-term growth view:** I position Dubai investment as a long-term play tied to the city’s real growth (population, capital inflows), not short-term speculation.

## Q9 — Macroeconomic indicators (GDP, GNP, NI; unemployment; inflation; macro policy)

### THEORY

**GDP (Gross Domestic Product)** — the total market value of all *final* goods and services produced *within a country* in a year (by domestic *and* foreign resources inside the borders). Excludes goods for further processing.

- **Expenditure approach:** GDP = Consumption + Investment + Government purchases + Net exports (C + I + G + Nx).
- **Income approach:** GDP = wages + interest + rents + profits (all income from production).

**NDP (Net Domestic Product)** = GDP − amortization (depreciation). Counts only *net* investment. **GNP (Gross National Product)** — total value of goods/services produced by a country’s **citizens**, wherever they are in the world. **NI (National Income)** = wages + rents + interest + corporate profits + proprietors’ income.

**Unemployment**

- *Unemployed* = no job in the reference period, actively looking, ready to work.
- *Labour force* (Ls) = employed (L) + unemployed (U); excludes children, students, retirees, and people not looking.
- *Unemployment rate* = U / Ls (× 100 for %).

**Types of unemployment:**

- **Voluntary** — won’t accept the market wage.
- **Involuntary** — would accept it, but there’s no demand.
- **Frictional** — short-term, people between/searching for jobs; part of the *natural* rate.
- **Structural** — long-term mismatch between workers’ skills/location and what firms need; needs retraining/relocation.
- **Cyclical** — caused by recession (falling total spending); the gap between actual and natural rate.

**Full employment** = when cyclical unemployment is zero (only natural rate remains). Frictional + structural are basically unavoidable.

**Inflation** — a rise in the general price level. Measured by:

- **CPI** (Consumer Price Index) — basket of common consumer goods.
- **PPI** (Producer Price Index) — input/producer prices.
- **GDP deflator** = nominal GDP / real GDP (nominal = current prices; real = base-year prices).

**Types of inflation:** moderate (0–9%) · galloping (10–99%) · hyperinflation (100%+) · inertial (self-sustaining, driven by expectations). **Sources:** **demand-pull** (AD rises faster than the economy can supply) · **cost-push** (input prices rise — e.g. an oil shock).

**Macroeconomic policy** — used to smooth the business cycle and control unemployment, inflation, and trade balance.

- **Fiscal policy** (government): *government spending* (raises total spending → affects GDP) and *taxes* (cut disposable income → slow GDP). Taxes can be flat or progressive.
- **Monetary policy** (central bank): controls the *money supply*. More money → lower interest rates → more investment/consumption. Less money → higher rates → less investment, lower GDP and inflation.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

*(Macro topic — lighter project link, but here are honest, speakable connections:)*

- **Inflation & purchasing power drive my clients:** when Czech inflation is high and people fear their savings losing value, **property abroad becomes attractive** as a store of value — that’s part of the demand for Dubai investment I’m tapping into.
- **Exchange rate / economic factors:** a Czech investor buying in Dubai is exposed to currency and to both economies’ health — part of the advice I give is reading that environment, not just the property.
- **German e-shop — inflation/price gap is the business model:** the whole e-shop exists because of **price differences between Czech and German markets**. Watching CPI-type price levels in both countries is literally how I judged whether the margin held.
- **Cost-push in real life:** rising shipping and input costs (a cost-push pressure) directly squeezed the e-shop’s margins and pricing decisions.

## Q11 — Applied Analytical Statistics

*Fundamental principle of mathematical statistics. Analysis of small and large data files. Basic procedure of statistical tests. Correlation coefficient. Parametric test in marketing research. Principle of regression analysis.*

### THEORY

**Principle of statistics.** We want to learn something about a large group, but we cannot measure everyone. So we take a smaller group from it, measure the property we care about, and use that to make a general statement about the whole group.

Key terms:

- **Population** — the whole set of elements we study.
- **Item (unit)** — one single element of the population.
- **Statistical variable** — the property we measure on each unit.
- **Sample** — a smaller subset selected from the population.
- **Data sample** — the set of values we actually collected.

**Large vs. small data files.**

- A **large data file** has roughly 50 values or more. We sort the values and group them into **classes (intervals)**. The way we sort depends on the variable type: *continuous* (takes any value in a range, e.g. height) or *discrete* (takes separate values, e.g. number of website visitors).
- A **small data file** has fewer values. We work out the **variation range R = max − min**, then split the axis into smaller classes that cover all the data. We can show the result as a **histogram** or a **polygon**.

**Basic procedure of a statistical test.** A statistical test is a tool for making a clear yes/no decision about a claim. The steps:

- State a **null hypothesis (H₀)** — the claim we are testing (usually “no effect” or “no difference”).
- State an **alternative hypothesis (H₁)** — the opposite.
- Choose a **level of significance (α)**, usually 0.05 (5%). This is the risk we accept of wrongly rejecting a true H₀.
- Calculate the test and its **p-value**.
- Decide: **if p-value < α → reject H₀** (the result is statistically significant). If **p-value ≥ α → we cannot reject H₀**.

⚠️ Common mistake to avoid: a *small* p-value means we **reject** the null hypothesis, not accept it. A small p-value = strong evidence against H₀.

**Correlation coefficient.** It measures how strong the relationship is between two variables. The most common one is the **Pearson** coefficient. It runs from −1 to +1:

- **+1** — perfect positive relationship (one goes up, the other goes up by a fixed proportion).
- **0** — no relationship (the variables are not linked).
- **−1** — perfect negative relationship (one goes up, the other goes down).

The closer the absolute value is to 1, the stronger the link. *Important:* correlation shows a relationship, not necessarily a cause.

**Parametric test + marketing research example.** A **parametric test** assumes the data follow a **normal distribution**. The most common are **t-tests** (one sample or two samples). The normal distribution is described by two parameters: the **mean (μ)** and the **standard deviation (σ)**.

- *Marketing research example:* we want to test a claim about one number against a known standard — “the market share of the new product will be over 15%,” or “at least 65% of people will like the product.” Each claim is a null hypothesis we test with a **one-sample test**.

**Regression analysis.** It lets us describe how one variable depends on another by fitting a suitable function to the data.

- **Simple regression** — one dependent and one independent variable.
- **Multiple regression** — one dependent variable affected by several independent variables.
- The **regression line** is the simplest fit. The **residual sum of squares** tells us how closely the line fits the real data (smaller = better fit). The random scatter not explained by the model is the **noise (error term)**.
- *Examples:* how a household’s food spending depends on the number of members; how a used car’s price depends on its age.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

- “In the thesis I worked with both small and large data sets. The 17 client interviews were a small qualitative sample. But the ad and web data — impressions, clicks, conversions — are large data files I can analyse statistically.”

## Q12 — Market Research

*Classification of research approaches (primary/secondary, qualitative/quantitative), research sources. Research methods. Description of the research process.*

### THEORY

**Market research** is the process of gathering, analysing and interpreting information about a market, a product, or a service. It studies present and potential customers — their characteristics, buying habits, location, and needs.

**By source of data:**

- **Primary research** — first-hand, fresh data collected directly from the source (surveys, focus groups, interviews). More expensive and slower, but exactly fits your problem.
- **Secondary research (desk research)** — data already collected by someone else (books, articles, company reports, statistics). Cheaper and faster, but not made for your exact question.

**By type of data:**

- **Quantitative research** — numerical, “hard” data analysed with maths or statistics. Uses **deduction** (first set a hypothesis, then collect data to test it). Results can be generalised. Answers “how many / how much.” Used to **confirm or test** something. *Methods:* surveys, sampling, statistical analysis.
- **Qualitative research** — describes **how and why** things happen. Uses **induction** (first collect data, then form the relationship). Helps you understand a problem in depth and in context. *Methods:* in-depth interviews, focus groups, observation.

**The research process (6 steps):**

- **Define the objective and problem** — what are we trying to solve? Form the research questions.
- **Determine the research design** — plan which methods to use, who is responsible, what information we need.
- **Design and prepare the research** — write the questions, set up the survey or experiment.
- **Sampling and data collection** — review secondary data first (cheaper), then collect primary data (asking, observing, surveying).
- **Analyse the data** — process what we collected and answer the research questions.
- **Visualise and interpret the results** — present findings, give recommendations, evaluate options.

## Q13 — Competitiveness: Competitive Space

*Competitive space as a framework. Understanding competition: definition, frame, triangle of relations, where/for what we compete, hierarchical definition of competitors, level of substitution, good and bad competitors, competitive maps.*

### THEORY

**Competitive space** is a way of understanding the market through three things: your **customers**, your **competitors**, and the **external factors** around them. The first crucial step is to define the customer clearly: *Who is the customer? Who is NOT the customer?*

**Concept of “Success-ability.”** This concept does not focus on defining customers but on **customer satisfaction**. It tells us to look at competition in the **broadest possible way** — not just rivals selling the same product.

**Frame of competition.** Because we look broadly, competition includes:

- **Competition to reach goals** — to achieve anything, you must overcome obstacles, which indirectly affects other companies and their goals.
- **Competition for inputs and outputs** — not only over selling products, but also over limited resources (good employees, materials, suppliers) that other companies also want.

**Triangle of competitive relations** — the relationship between *us, our competitors, and the customer* — every competitive move happens inside this triangle.

**Hierarchical definition of competitors (levels of competition):**

- **Confrontational level** — reaching your goals while actively limiting the competition.
- **Self-realization level** — reaching your own goals without limiting others.
- **Qualification level** — building the basic ability you need just to be allowed into the competition.

**Level of substitution (a general principle).** How closely a rival can replace you:

- **Brand competition** — different firms offering a similar product at a similar price.
- **Segment competition** — different firms offering the same *type* of product.
- **Service competition** — different firms satisfying the same customer *need*.
- **Overall competition** — competition among all companies for the customer’s money.

**Good vs. bad competitors:**

- **Good competitor** — has a clear strategy, respects the rules, is stable and successful. Competing with them pushes you forward and makes the market healthier.
- **Bad competitor** — does not respect the rules, is usually average or below average. Even when they “win,” it tends to damage the market rather than improve it.

**Competitive maps.** A framework that compares two (or more) competitors on **two axes** — for example price vs. quality, or product range vs. ability to innovate. It shows visually where everyone sits and where there is an open gap.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

- “I used **competitive maps / perceptual positioning** to plot competitors on two axes — for example *trust / personal guidance* vs. *price*. Most competitors cluster as cheap-but-impersonal sellers, which left an open space for a trusted, advisory, consultative position. That gap is where I positioned the company.”
- “The **mystery shopping** I did was exactly competitive comparison — I experienced a key competitor’s full sales process and benchmarked it against what I wanted to deliver.”
- “On substitution: my real competition isn’t only other Dubai advisors (brand competition). It’s also any other investment a client could choose instead (overall competition) — so I’m competing for the client’s *decision*, not just against similar firms.”

## Q14 — Strategic Competitiveness

*Vision and 3D view of objectives. Competitive base of strategy: Porter’s strategies, Success-Ability concept, competitive advantage and its typology. Development of advantage: value creation, value chain, value proposition, smile curve.*

### THEORY

**Vision of the company** is made of three parts:

- **Mission** — the way the company behaves (its purpose).
- **Idea** — what determines the company’s direction and behaviour.
- **Vision** — the direction the company is heading in.

**3D view of objectives.** Every goal of a company fits into one of three dimensions, and the aim is to keep them in **balance**:

- **Economic** — goals from the owners’ and management’s point of view (revenue, EBIT, margin, market share).
- **External** — goals about the environment the company operates in; based on long-term strategy and on the outside groups that affect success.
- **Internal** — goals inside the company (employee satisfaction, safety, technology, efficient processes), which you need in order to reach the economic and external goals.

**Porter’s competitive strategies.** Four generic strategies, all aimed at **competitive advantage**. Porter identifies two ways to compete — **lower cost** or **differentiation** — and either a **broad** or a **narrow (niche)** target:

- **Cost leadership** (broad + cost) — be the cheapest to produce/deliver, so you stay more profitable.
- **Differentiation** (broad + different) — make your product clearly different from rivals.
- **Cost focus** (niche + cost) — be the low-cost option inside a narrow niche.
- **Differentiation focus** (niche + different) — be the special, differentiated option inside a narrow niche.

**Success-Ability concept** — describes a company’s ability to succeed, built from: its **goals**, its **competitiveness**, the **external factors**, and how it **achieves goals and develops** in the future.

**Typology of competitive advantage:**

- **Pure competitive advantage** — real company resources: *tangible* (machinery, finance, location), *intangible*(software, patents, know-how), *HR* (employees’ knowledge, experience, motivation), *abilities* (methods and approaches).
- **Semi competitive advantage** — *acceptable* (government help, strong partners, natural monopoly) or *unidentifiable*(needs detailed analysis to classify).
- **Pseudo competitive advantage** — gained by breaking ethical or legal rules (not sustainable).

**Development of advantage:**

- **Value creation** — the core of business; using resources (land, machinery, labour, materials) to create something worth more than the inputs. The easiest measure of value creation is **revenue**, not profit.
- **Value chain** — the set of activities a company does to deliver a valuable product. Mapping it shows where to cut cost or add value, raising the overall margin.
- **Value proposition** — the value the company promises to deliver. It answers *“****why should the customer buy from us?****”* and states the clearest benefit.
- **Smile curve** — shows that value is not added equally along the chain. The **ends** (concept/R&D/branding at the start, and marketing/distribution/service at the end) add the **most** value; the **middle** (manufacturing/assembly) adds the **least** — so the graph looks like a smile.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

- “I built the **value proposition** explicitly — I used the **Value Proposition Canvas** in the thesis. The promise is: we remove the fear and information gap of buying property abroad and guide you safely from interest to a closed deal.”
- “On value creation — the company turns expertise, market access and developer relationships into a service worth far more than its inputs. The value chain runs from attention (ads) → education (landing page) → trust (nurturing) → consultative call → closing with the developer.”
- “I also used **Blue Ocean / ERRC** to step out of the red-ocean price competition and create a new, less contested space — advisory quality rather than cheapest deal.”

## Q15 — Branding in a Small Company

*Technical/financial/tempting aspects, brand elements, brand architecture, eight aspects of branding. Magical chain. Brand identity: brand prism, positioning, personality, communication, brand personality ring, brand performance, consistency. Brand development levels.*

### THEORY

A successful brand has **three aspects**: **technical** (it works), **tempting** (it attracts and appeals), and **financial** (it makes money).

**Brand elements:** name, label, business page, logo, package design, motto, colour.

**Brand architecture** (how brands relate to each other in a company):

- **Corporate brand** — one master brand for everything.
- **Connected brand** — sub-brands linked to the parent.
- **Independent brands** — separate brands that don’t show the parent.

**Eight aspects of branding in successful companies:** function, image, style, extension, emotion, self-expression, story, identity.

**Magical chain** — an approach that links **location, company, brand, product features, and distribution** into one meaningful chain: *country → company → brand → product → substitute → store.* You don’t always need every link — only the ones that create a meaningful connection.

**Brand identity — Kapferer’s Brand Prism.** Six connected elements that build and bound the brand:

- **Physique** — the brand’s physical, tangible features.
- **Personality** — the character the brand shows; what kind of person it would be.
- **Culture** — the brand’s core values, shown through communication.
- **Relationship** — how the brand treats customers (service, care).
- **Reflection** — the typical customer the brand seems to address (e.g. young, independent professionals).
- **Self-image** — how customers see *themselves* when using the brand.

**Brand identity also defined by three questions:**

- **Positioning** — the mix of attributes the brand offers → **WHAT**. Key attributes: differentiating (biggest part), supporting, competitive, qualitative.
- **Personality** — characteristics with an emotional base → **WHO**.
- **Communication** — informing the market about positioning and personality → **HOW**.

**Brand personality ring** — five personality groups: **sincerity, excitement, competence, sophistication, ruggedness**(each with sub-traits, e.g. excitement = trendy, cool, young, imaginative).

**Brand performance — building a strong brand needs:**

- **Consistency** — stable and predictable.
- **Understandability** — easy to grasp what the brand says.
- **Harmony** — all parts match, no internal conflict.

**Brand development levels:**

- **Strategic** — the long-term plan and the goals (tied to customer needs, emotions, competition).
- **Tactical** — the medium-term details and steps to reach the strategic goal.
- **Operational** — the daily activities that make the branding actually happen.

## Q16 — Service Management (Services & the 7Ps)

*Characteristics of services, types of service industries, service mix categories, impact and trends. The 7Ps applied to services.*

### THEORY

**A service** is any act or performance one party offers another that is **intangible**. Services are usually tied closely to a good. Key characteristics:

- **Intangible** — you can’t touch them before buying.
- **Cannot be stored or transported.**
- **Inseparable** — they come into existence when bought/consumed (produced and consumed at the same time).
- **Perishable** — they “expire” instantly (an empty seat on a flight is lost forever).
- **Variable** — quality changes with who delivers it and when.

**Impact:** the service sector is the **biggest sector** of the world economy — more than 60% of global wealth. The trend, pushed by COVID, is **digitalisation** and moving services online.

**Service industries:** government (education, hospitals, postal), nonprofit (museums, charities), business (banks, hotels, insurance, consulting), manufacturing (in-house accountants, legal, IT staff), retail (cashiers, salespeople, customer service).

**Categories of the service mix** (from most goods to most service):

- **Pure tangible goods** — soap, salt.
- **Tangible goods with accompanying services** — the more advanced the product, the more support it needs.
- **Hybrid** — a restaurant meal (food + service equally).
- **Major service with minor goods** — a flight that includes a meal.
- **Pure service** — babysitting, massage.

**The 7Ps for services:**

- **Product (the service)** — the company’s performance for the customer; an experience that must be planned and controlled. Layers: *core benefit → basic product → expected product → augmented product → potential product.*
- **Price** — high variability (the same service can cost different amounts for different clients); pricing can be cost-based, competition-based, or demand-based (what the customer will pay).
- **Place (distribution)** — how the service reaches the customer; can be offline or online (app, e-shop, showroom, pop-up).
- **Promotion** — communicating the service and overcoming its intangibility (personal selling, advertising, word of mouth, social media).
- **People** — the **most important** P. Frontline employees *are* the service — they are the first contact and build the experience.
- **Process** — the path of the service from company to customer (ordering, payment, support).
- **Physical evidence** — because the service is intangible, the physical surroundings (office, materials, website look) act as proof of quality and help the customer decide.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

## Q17 — Service Failure, Recovery & Service Quality

*Service failure and recovery steps, service (recovery) paradox. Customer services: characteristics, examples, importance. Services from manufacturers. Service quality, its dimensions and measurement.*

### THEORY

**Service failure** — when the company does not meet the customer’s expectations. The result can be a lost customer and lost trust. *Causes:* inexperienced or rude staff, delays, slow delivery — many of them are inside the company’s control.

**Service recovery — simple steps to fix it:**

- **Listen and apologise.**
- **Fix the problem fast** and offer alternatives.
- **Do something extra** to please the customer.
- **Give the customer full attention.**
- **Make sure the customer leaves satisfied.**

**Service recovery paradox** — a customer can think *more* highly of the company *after* a good recovery than if nothing had gone wrong. A well-handled fix builds confidence and trust.

- *Example:* a flight is cancelled, the airline offers a chosen replacement flight, a meal voucher while waiting, and a discount coupon — the customer ends up impressed.

**Customer service.** To satisfy customers, you first need **satisfied employees**, because they are the face of the company. Customer service is the core of business: meeting expectations and needs. The most important part is the **human element**— empathy, friendliness, patience, commitment.

- *Examples:* a thank-you note after a purchase, remembering a regular’s usual order, sending a survey after the service.

**Customer service from manufacturers** — extra activities that complement a physical product and differentiate it: special transport/packaging, guarantee repairs, free after-purchase repairs, good payment conditions.

**Dimensions of service quality (RATER):**

- **Reliability** — delivering what was promised.
- **Responsiveness** — willingness to help quickly and adapt.
- **Assurance** — staff knowledge that builds trust.
- **Empathy** — caring, individual attention.
- **Tangibles** — physical facilities and equipment.

**Measuring service quality:**

- **SERVQUAL** — measures the **gap** between customer **expectations** and customer **perception**, across the five dimensions above. Used for internal management, benchmarking and segmentation.
- **SERVPERF** — 22 statements measuring satisfaction by the service actually performed (perception only).
- **Blueprinting** — maps the whole service process to find weak points where failures are likely, so you can prevent them.

## Q18 — Digital Marketing, Marketing Funnel & Social Media

**Role of digital marketing in a small company.** For little money you can reach many potential customers — usually cheaper than traditional marketing. - **Decision** — buy or not (note: the decider may not be the user or the buyer).
- **Purchase** — the real purchase; keep checkout and payment easy.
- **Repeat** — the customer buys again. *Tools:* after-sales service, CRM, follow-up email.
- **Loyalty** — they prefer your brand. *Tools:* community, shared values.

- **SEE** — reach the widest relevant audience who don’t know you yet. *Media:* social media, SEO, blogs, YouTube.

- **DO** — they are ready to buy; convince them you’re the best. *Media:* PPC, email, SEO, affiliate.
- **CARE** — look after existing customers and turn them loyal. *Media:* social, direct email, customer service.

**Social media — functionalities:** being visible online (a must for B2C), building brand awareness and community, understanding customers, reaching a large audience with organic content, and measuring your performance against competitors.

**Social media — benefits:** building relationships, more visibility, faster and easier communication, more website traffic, customer feedback, and tracking the competition.

## Q19 — SEO, SEM & Paid Campaigns

*SEO and organic network; off-page SEO (links, social media’s indirect influence); on-page SEO (title tag, meta keywords, meta description); web structure (domains, subdomains, navigation, URL); content marketing (anchor/heading text, keywords). Search Engine Marketing, paid campaigns, structure and metrics.*

### THEORY

**SEO (Search Engine Optimization)** — improving how visible your website is in the **organic (unpaid)** search results, so your page shows near the top for a chosen keyword. Roughly **80% of clicks go to organic** results and only ~20% to paid ads.

**On-page SEO** (things on your own pages):

- **Title tag** — unique, descriptive, with keywords; it’s the headline the user clicks.
- **Meta keywords** — now carry little weight, but still flag your focus keywords.
- **Meta description** — the short page description shown in results; should be unique per page.
- **Web structure** — **URL** (keep it short and descriptive), **domain** (your unique address/name), **subdomains** (can look more authoritative and hold keywords), **navigation** (logical so users find things — this is *usability*).
- **Image alt text, media descriptions, file names** — extra places to add keywords and help search engines understand the content.

**Content marketing:**

- **Anchor text** — the visible clickable text of a link; should contain keywords.
- **Heading tags** — show the headline hierarchy on the page.
- **Keywords** — the most important factor; the more relevant they are, the higher you rank.

**Off-page SEO** (things outside your site):

- **Links** — still the single biggest factor in search ranking. **Link value** = the quality of a link, based on the relevance and trust of the linking site.
- **Social media’s indirect influence** — posts and profiles appear in search results and get your site noticed; cheap to start, easy, fast.

**SEM (Search Engine Marketing)** — online marketing that gets your page to the top of search, including **paid** ads.

**Structure of an online (paid) campaign:**

- Define the customer.
- Choose the target.
- Set the budget.
- Create the ad content.
- Track and monitor.

**Metrics of paid campaigns:**

- **CTR (click-through rate)** — clicks ÷ impressions.
- **CPI / CPM (cost per impression / per thousand)** — what you pay per impression.
- **CPC (cost per click)** — what you pay for one click.
- **PPC (pay-per-click)** — the model where you pay per click; lets you cap spending.
- **Conversion** — when a user does the action you wanted (purchase, sign-up, download).
- **Conversion rate** — % of visitors who completed the goal (higher = better).
- **Conversion path** — the steps a visitor takes to become a customer.
- **Abandonment rate** — how many people left before finishing.

## Q1 — Planning

*Definition, types of planning and plans, planning process.*

### THEORY

**Planning** is the management function of setting objectives and deciding the actions to reach them. You need a clear view of the company’s environment and the ability to adapt to changing future conditions. It is one of the core project and time-management techniques, and it reduces the time, effort and cost of reaching a goal.

- **Forecasting** = predicting what the future *will* look like.
- **Planning** = deciding what the future *should* look like, and preparing for several scenarios.

**Planning process (result-oriented, 3 steps — Montana & Charnov):**

- **Choose a destination** — collect and analyse information (identify the problem, list resources, set objectives).
- **Evaluate alternative routes** — form alternatives, weigh them, and make the decision.
- **Decide the specific course** — implement the plan and evaluate it.

**Organizational planning process (5 steps):**

- Develop the **strategic** plan (aligned with mission, vision, values).
- Turn it into a **tactical** plan (measurable, time-bound goals).
- Create **operational** plans (what and when).
- **Execute** the plans.
- **Monitor** progress and adjust.

**Types of planning (by level and time):**

- **Strategic** — long term (3+ years); sets direction and mobilises resources; based on the mission and on analysis of opportunities/threats and strengths/weaknesses.
- **Tactical** — short term (≈1 year); how the company will carry out the strategic plan; low-level managers.
- **Operational** — day-to-day milestones and conditions for success; the basis for the annual operating budget (1–3 years).
- **Contingency** — backup plans for when the primary plan fails.

- “In the cooperative we planned at all three levels: a **strategic** direction for what each project team would build, **tactical** goals for the year, and **operational** day-to-day tasks. As members we had to forecast and prepare for several scenarios, not just one.”

## Q2 — Organizing

*Organization as activity, organizational structure, types of structures, the organizing process, responsibility and authority, delegation. Specifics in a small company.*

### THEORY

**Organizing** is the management function that follows planning. It means assigning tasks, grouping them into departments, and allocating resources so the company can reach the goals set in the plan. In short: building an effective system of authority across management levels.

**The organizing process (4 steps):**

- **Identify and divide the work** → specialisation.
- **Group the jobs into departments** → the organizational structure.
- **Assign duties** → each person’s responsibilities (job description).
- **Establish reporting relationships** → who reports to whom (the chain of command and managerial levels).

**Authority and delegation.**

- **Authority** — the right to make decisions; top managers hold the most. Clear authority means everyone knows who they report to.
- **Delegation of authority** — passing a task (and the right to act on it) to another employee, so deadlines and objectives are met.

**Types of organizational structure:**

- **Functional** — group people by similar tasks/functions; the most traditional type; easy collaboration within a function.
- **Divisional** — group by product, service, or geographic area; each division has its own resources.
- **Matrix** — a hybrid of functional and divisional; employees answer to two bosses (a functional manager and a project manager); roles are flexible, so priorities must be clear.
- **Flat (horizontal)** — few or no management layers; fast communication and decisions; common in small companies.

**Organizing in a small company** (no one-size-fits-all):

- Create departments around the three things every business needs: **marketing/sales, operations, finance**.
- Fill each department with its tasks.
- Assign a person to lead each one.
- Define the structure.

## Q4 — Control

*Definition, control process, control standards, deviations, types of control.*

### THEORY

**Control** is the management function that checks for errors so they can be corrected and the company’s goals reached. It compares **actual performance** against the **standards and plans**, points out mistakes early, and is always **action-oriented**. It is closely tied to planning.

**Control process (3 steps):**

- **Set standards** — what good performance looks like.
- **Measure actual performance.**
- **Compare** actual vs. standard and act on the difference.
- **Control standard** — the benchmark against which performance is measured.
- **Control deviation** — when actual performance differs from the standard; good internal control prevents these.

**Types of control:**

- **Feedback control (post-action)** — review what already happened to improve next time.
- **Concurrent control (real-time)** — monitor and adjust an activity *while* it runs.
- **Feedforward / preliminary control** — check inputs *before* the activity starts to prevent problems. *(added for completeness — the classic third ****“timing”**** type alongside feedback and concurrent)*
- **Behavioural control** — evaluate the decision-making and behaviour of staff, not just results.
- **Financial control** — control costs, resources and expenses against the budget.

## Q5 — Decision Making

*Process and steps, decision-making styles (autocratic, analytical, behavioral, conceptual), factors affecting decisions (certainty, risk, uncertainty).*

### THEORY

**Decision making** means identifying a goal, gathering the relevant information, and choosing between alternatives. The first and most important step is to recognise that a decision is actually needed and why — it should serve a larger company goal.

**Decision-making process (5 steps):**

- **Identify the goal** — what must be decided.
- **Gather information** and list all alternatives.
- **Evaluate** risks, consequences and criteria.
- **Make and execute** the decision.
- **Evaluate** the outcome afterwards.

**Decision-making styles:**

- **Autocratic (directive)** — one person decides with no input from others. *+ fast, clear responsibility; − one person’s mistakes affect everyone.*
- **Analytical** — uses weighted criteria in a hierarchy; the most important issues count most; common in academic/policy settings.
- **Behavioural** — focuses on relationships and people’s feelings; team-based, high social focus.
- **Conceptual** — explores big ideas and creative options; future-oriented; good for long-term decisions.

**Factors affecting the decision (how much you know):**

- **Certainty** — you know all alternatives and outcomes in advance.
- **Risk** — you have only partial information; outcomes have known probabilities.
- **Uncertainty** — probabilities and outcomes are unknown; you rely on intuition and experience.

## Q7 — Assets and Capital Structure (Balance Sheet)

*Balance sheet, asset structure, capital structure, optimal capital structure.*

### THEORY

**Capital structure** = the mix of **debt and equity** a company uses to finance its operations and growth — i.e. how the company funds itself.

**Balance sheet** — a financial statement showing what a company **owns (assets)**, **owes (liabilities)**, and the **owners’ equity**, at one point in time. The basic rule: **Assets = Liabilities + Owners’ Equity** (the two sides must always balance).

**Asset structure:**

- **Fixed (non-current) assets** — intangible, tangible, and long-term financial assets.
- **Current assets** — inventories, receivables (short- and long-term), short-term financial assets (cash).
- **Accruals** — timing adjustments.

**Capital (liabilities + equity) structure:**

- **Owners’ equity** — common stock, capital funds, funds from profit, the economic result (profit or loss).
- **Liabilities** — reserves, long-term debt, short-term debt, bank loans.

**Optimal capital structure** — the best mix of debt and equity that **maximises the company’s value while minimising the cost of capital**. A healthy structure usually has **lower debt and more equity** — a strong balance sheet that signals quality to investors. (Some debt is fine, because debt is cheaper than equity and interest is tax-deductible, but too much raises risk.)

## Q8 — Revenues and Costs (Profit & Loss, Break-Even)

*Profit and loss statement, accounting vs. economic profit, classification of costs, break-even analysis.*

### THEORY

- **Revenues** — total money received for goods/services sold in a period.
- **Costs of revenue** — total cost of making and delivering those goods/services.

**Profit and loss (P&L) statement** — summarises revenues, costs and expenses over a period to show whether the company made a **profit or a loss**. Every public company issues it alongside the balance sheet and cash-flow statement. It can be organised **by nature of costs** (personnel, depreciation, operating costs) or **by purpose** (what the costs were used for).

**Accounting vs. economic profit** (corrected):

- **Accounting profit = revenues − explicit (accounting) costs.** Only real, paid-out costs.
- **Economic profit = revenues − explicit costs − opportunity (implicit) costs.** It also subtracts what you gave up (e.g. interest you could have earned on your own capital, your own unpaid time). So economic profit is always ≤ accounting profit.

⚠️ Correction vs. the draft: accounting profit does **not** include opportunity costs — those are subtracted only in **economic** profit.

**Classification of costs:** by purpose, by origin/responsibility, calculational (per unit), and for decision-making (e.g. fixed vs. variable, direct vs. indirect).

**Break-even analysis** — how many units you must sell to cover all costs. The **break-even point** is where total costs = total revenues (no profit, no loss):

**BEP (units) = Fixed costs ÷ (Sales price per unit − Variable cost per unit)**

The bracket is the *contribution margin* per unit — what each sale contributes to covering fixed costs.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

## Q9 — Company Financing

*Sources of funds, short-term financing, long-term financing, asset-funding strategies.* *(Not in the draft — written fresh.)*

### THEORY

**Company financing** means obtaining the money a business needs to start, run and grow. Sources are classified two ways.

**By ownership:**

- **Internal (own) financing** — generated inside the company: retained profit, depreciation, sale of assets.
- **External financing** — from outside: equity (owners, investors) or debt (loans).

**By maturity:**

- **Short-term financing** (up to ~1 year) — funds the day-to-day running and working capital. Examples: supplier (trade) credit, short-term bank loans, overdrafts, factoring.
- **Long-term financing** (over ~1 year) — funds fixed assets and growth. Examples: equity/share capital, retained earnings, long-term bank loans, bonds, leasing.

**By source type — equity vs. debt:**

- **Equity** — owners’ money or investors; no obligation to repay, but owners share control and profit. More expensive in the long run.
- **Debt** — borrowed money repaid with interest; keeps control, interest is tax-deductible, but adds fixed obligations and risk.

**Asset-funding strategies** (how you match financing maturity to asset maturity):

- **Conservative** — use long-term financing even for part of current assets. Safe, low risk, but more expensive.
- **Aggressive** — use short-term financing for some fixed/permanent assets. Cheaper, but risky (you must keep refinancing).
- **Matching (moderate)** — fund long-term assets with long-term money and short-term assets with short-term money. The balanced “golden rule” of financing.

## Q10 — Financial Statements and the Financial Plan

*Balance sheet, P&L, cash flow; preparing a cash-flow statement; projected financial statements; short-term vs. long-term financial plan.* *(Not in the draft — written fresh.)*

### THEORY

**The three core financial statements:**

- **Balance sheet** — what the company owns and owes at a point in time (Assets = Liabilities + Equity).
- **Profit & loss (income) statement** — revenues minus costs over a period → profit or loss.
- **Cash-flow statement** — the actual movement of cash in and out over a period. *Profit is not the same as cash* — a company can be profitable but run out of cash, which is why this statement matters.

**Cash-flow statement — three parts:**

- **Operating** — cash from the core business (sales, paying suppliers and staff).
- **Investing** — cash from buying/selling assets.
- **Financing** — cash from loans, repayments, owner contributions, dividends.

**Preparing a cash-flow statement — two methods:**

- **Direct method** — list actual cash receipts and payments.
- **Indirect method** — start from net profit and adjust for non-cash items (e.g. add back depreciation) and changes in working capital.

**Financial plan** — projects the statements into the future (projected/“pro-forma” balance sheet, P&L and cash flow) to see whether the plan is financially viable.

- **Short-term financial plan** (up to 1 year) — detailed; focuses on cash flow and liquidity (will I have enough cash next month?).
- **Long-term financial plan** (several years) — strategic; focuses on funding growth, investments and capital structure.

## Q11 — Financing the Business & Working Capital

*Short-term and long-term financing sources; meaning of working capital; basics of working-capital management.* *(Not in the draft — written fresh.)*

### THEORY

**Financing sources** (see also Q9):

- **Short-term sources** (≤1 year) — trade/supplier credit, overdrafts, short-term loans, factoring; used mainly to fund **working capital**.
- **Long-term sources** (>1 year) — equity, retained earnings, long-term loans, bonds, leasing; used to fund **fixed assets** and growth.

**Working capital** = **Current Assets − Current Liabilities.** It is the money tied up in running the business day to day — the buffer that lets you pay short-term bills.

- **Current assets:** cash, receivables (money owed by customers), inventory.
- **Current liabilities:** payables (money you owe suppliers), short-term loans.
- **Positive working capital** = you can cover short-term obligations; **negative** = a liquidity warning.

**Working-capital management** — managing the short-term assets and liabilities to stay liquid *without* tying up too much cash. The main levers:

- **Inventory** — hold enough to trade, but not so much that cash is frozen.
- **Receivables** — collect from customers quickly.
- **Payables** — use the supplier payment terms you’re given (without damaging the relationship).

The shorter the **cash conversion cycle** (time from paying for inputs to collecting from customers), the less working capital you need.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

The **German e-shop and dropshipping** are *ideal* working-capital examples.

Because an external supplier holds and ships the stock, I carried **little or no inventory** — so almost no cash was frozen in goods. The customer often pays before I pay the supplier, which means a **very short (even negative) cash conversion cycle**.” *(reasonable assumption — confirm the exact payment timing)*
- “That’s working-capital management at its best: maximise liquidity, minimise tied-up cash. It’s also why these models can run on very little financing.”
- “On **Dubai**, working capital is small because it’s a service — no inventory at all. My main short-term need is funding ad spend before commissions arrive, which is the one working-capital gap I plan around.”

## Q12 — Capital Budgeting

*Methods of capital budgeting and their interpretation.*

### THEORY

**Capital budgeting** is how a business **evaluates major, usually long-term investments** before approving or rejecting them. It answers one question: *Is this project financially worthwhile?* (Examples: building a new plant, a big outside investment.)

**Methods:**

- **Payback period** — how long until the initial investment is recovered from net cash flows. *Simple and fast, but ignores cash flows after the cut-off and the time value of money.*
- **Discounted payback period** — same idea, but **discounts** future cash flows, so it respects the time value of money. *Still ignores cash flows after the cut-off.*
- **Net Present Value (NPV)** — discounts **all** the project’s cash flows to today and subtracts the investment. *The most economically sound method.*
- **NPV = present value of cash inflows − cash outflows.** If **NPV > 0 → accept**; if **NPV < 0 → reject**.
- **Profitability Index (PI)** — NPV expressed as a ratio; useful when choosing between projects of different sizes.
- **PI = present value of future cash flows ÷ initial investment.** If **PI > 1 → accept** (benefits exceed costs).
- *(Often also mentioned: IRR — Internal Rate of Return, the discount rate at which NPV = 0; accept if IRR ****>**** the required return.)*

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

**Dubai** turns capital budgeting into a marketing-investment decision.

- “I apply capital-budgeting logic to the choice of investing in **paid acquisition**. Spending on ads and the system is an investment, and the ‘project’ is worthwhile if the commissions it brings exceed the cost — that’s an **NPV/payback** mindset applied to client acquisition.”
- “**Payback period** is intuitive here: how many closed deals until the campaign pays for itself? Once I’m past break-even, every further deal is return on that investment.”

- “On a **bigger** outlay like building the website, I’d weigh it the same way — upfront cost now against the leads and deals it should generate over time (NPV thinking).”

## Q13 — Project Approach, Project Life Cycle, Program & Portfolio

### THEORY

**Project management** is the part of management that directs and delivers **projects**. A **project** is **temporary and unique**, with a defined **start and end**, creating something new.

**Project life cycle (5 phases):**

- **Start-up** — define objectives, agree the concept, identify the problem.
- **Definition** — assess risks, finance and benefits; define the scope in detail.
- **Planning** — break the project into areas; plan time, cost and resources (continuous).
- **Execution** — do the work; control and monitor it.
- **Close-out** — hand over, review afterwards, capture lessons.

**Project vs. program vs. portfolio:**

- **Project** — one temporary, unique piece of work.
- **Program** — a group of **related** projects, managed together; done when all its projects finish.
- **Portfolio** — the organization’s whole **collection** of projects and programs, managed as a group to meet strategic objectives.
- Flow: **projects → program → portfolio.** Project, program and portfolio managers must work together for strategic success.

## Q14 — Stakeholder Management, Risk, Project Charter, Logical Framework

### THEORY

**Stakeholder** — any party with an interest in the project who can **affect it or be affected by it**. Primary stakeholders: investors, employees, customers, suppliers.

**Project organizational structure** — sets the hierarchy, roles and reporting for a project. Typically one **project manager** above the project, with leaders below (e.g. hardware, software, production). A clear structure lowers risk and helps reach the goal.

**Risk management (4 steps):**

- **Identify** — list all risks with a negative impact; ask all stakeholders.
- **Assess** — judge each risk’s impact and likelihood to set priorities.
- **Monitor** — track the plan; check which risks occurred or were missed.
- **Report** — keep stakeholders updated.

**Project charter** — the document that authorises the project to start (signed at the beginning). Contains: title, project manager, start/end dates, scope, deliverables, risk analysis, milestone schedule, and the team.

**Logical framework matrix (LogFrame)** — a one-page overview of the project’s key objectives, activities, resources, and the external **assumptions** that could affect it. It gives a clear, logical summary plus risk and implementation notes.

## Q15 — Tools and Techniques in Project Management

*Triple imperative, WBS, RACI, critical path/network charts, Gantt chart, Earned Value Management.*

### THEORY

**Triple imperative (the project triangle)** — three linked constraints: **Time, Cost, Scope**. Changing one affects the others. **Quality** is not a side of the triangle but the overall objective.

**Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)** — breaks a complex project into a **hierarchy of smaller parts**, so work can be done in parallel and the project becomes manageable. Each level adds more detail.

**RACI matrix** — clarifies roles to avoid confusion:

- **R – Responsible** — does the work.
- **A – Accountable** — owns correct completion (usually one person/sponsor).
- **C – Consulted** — experts who give input.
- **I – Informed** — kept updated.

**Critical path / network chart** — the **longest** sequence of dependent tasks from start to finish; if any task on it slips, the **whole project is delayed**. A network diagram visualises the schedule and dependencies.

**Gantt chart** — a bar chart of tasks over time (time on the horizontal axis, tasks on the vertical). Shows what is scheduled when, who is responsible, and the project’s start and end.

**Earned Value Management (EVM)** — integrates scope, schedule and cost to measure performance against the plan:

- **Planned Value (PV)** — budgeted cost of work scheduled.
- **Actual Cost (AC)** — what the work actually cost.
- **Earned Value (EV)** — budgeted cost of work actually performed.
- **Schedule variance** (− behind, + ahead, 0 on time) and **cost variance** show how far you are from the baseline.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

**Dubai’s** launch (and the thesis) is a natural place to apply these.

- “The **triple imperative** describes my reality exactly: building the system, I trade off **time, cost and scope**, with **quality** (a service clients trust) as the real goal. Pausing campaigns shifts the *time* side while protecting *quality*.”
- “I’d use a **WBS** to split the launch into manageable parts — website, ad creative, landing page, CRM setup — so my collaborators can work in parallel.”
- “A **RACI** matrix fits well with external collaborators: I’m **Accountable** for the whole project, the video/web/marketing people are **Responsible** for their pieces, and I keep developers **Informed**.”
- “A **Gantt chart** would map the launch timeline, and the **critical path** would be the sequence that can’t slip — e.g. the landing page must be live before ads can run.”

## Q16 — Agile Approach & SCRUM

### THEORY

**Agile project management** — an **iterative** approach that delivers a project in small steps through its life cycle. It focuses on delivering **maximum value** within the time and budget available, and on **collaboration**, especially with the customer. Most common in software development.

**SCRUM** — a process framework for managing complex product development through adaptive, iterative work. **Core values:** courage, focus, commitment, respect, openness.

**SCRUM — roles, events, artifacts:**

- **Roles (the Scrum Team — no sub-teams or hierarchy):**
- *Product Owner* — owns the product backlog and priorities (the value).
- *Scrum Master* — keeps the process running, removes blockers.

- **Events** (fixed time-boxes that create rhythm and cut meetings): *Sprint* (the container), *Sprint Planning*, *Daily Scrum*, *Sprint Review*, *Sprint Retrospective*.
- **Artifacts** (provide transparency): *Product Backlog* (all the work), *Sprint Backlog* (work for this sprint), *Increment* (the finished, usable result).

## Q17 — Personnel Strategy, Planning, Recruitment, Selection, Adaptation, Development

### THEORY

**Personnel strategy** sets the direction, objectives and principles for managing people, matching existing employees to the company’s long-term goals. It asks: *Who do we employ? What skills do we need? Can we develop them?*

**Main HR functions:**

- **HR planning** — continuously forecasting the **type and number** of workers needed, to get the best fit between people and jobs, balancing short-term needs with long-term conditions.
- **Recruitment and selection** — finding candidates and picking the right one. *Process:* identify the need → write the job description → advertise → manage responses → shortlist → interview → select.
- **Adaptation (onboarding)** — introducing the new employee to the company, team and tasks; first impressions and early motivation matter. Includes **social adaptation** (fitting into the group) and **occupational adaptation** (mastering the tasks). Closely tied to **corporate culture** (shared values, language, structure, benefits).
- **Development and training** — linking company goals to each person’s growth; tailored to the individual; communicated early to boost satisfaction and motivation. *Options:* in-house training, public/education seminars, e-learning.

## Q18 — Motivation, Performance Evaluation, Remuneration, Dismissal

### THEORY

**Motivation** — a universal but individual psychological process: getting people to do what’s needed because *they want to*. It runs: **unsatisfied need → drive → satisfaction.** A **motivation system** is the chosen set of motivators used to encourage performance. *(Maslow’s hierarchy — see Q3.)*

**Performance evaluation** — a formal, systematic process (usually yearly) that measures an employee’s work against their job description. Its purpose is **feedback**: confirm strengths, target weak areas, improve communication and team performance, and ultimately raise customer satisfaction.

**Remuneration** — the reward/compensation for work performed; a basic attraction that drives effort and motivation.

**Employee dismissal (termination of employment)** — ways it ends: mutual agreement, a fixed-term contract ending, expiry, or a serious reason (e.g. breaking workplace rules).

- **Notice period** — at least **2 months** in the Czech Republic, starting the first day of the month after notice is given.
- **Severance pay** — when employment ends for **organizational reasons** (redundancy), the amount depends on length of service (roughly **1× / 2× / 3×** average monthly earnings for under 1 / 1–2 / 2+ years). For termination due to a **work injury or occupational disease**, severance is at least **12×** average monthly earnings.

## Q19 — Workforce Analysis, Job Design, Competency Modeling

### THEORY

**Workforce analysis** — a framework for personnel decisions, ensuring the **right people with the right skills are in the right place at the right time**. It uses a **workforce profile**: number of employees, locations, job classifications, education/experience, salary levels, gender, contract types.

**Job design** — dividing work tasks and assigning them to individuals: it specifies **what** an employee does, **how**, and **why**. Good job design supports organizational goals, motivation and satisfaction. A **job description** covers duties, required skills, wage, and department.

**Competency model** — an HR guideline defining the specific **skills, knowledge and behaviours** an employee needs to succeed in a role; it describes what success looks like.

- **Difference:** a **job description** is a general summary of required skills; a **competency model** specifies the **behaviours** the employee must show to succeed.
- **Benefits:** clear direction for performance, better understanding of staff abilities, more accurate identification of learning needs, and a consistent basis for evaluation.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

The **cooperative** shows job design; **Dubai** shows competency thinking.

## Q3 — Small Business Management Style & Growth Model

*Owner/manager model, advantages/disadvantages, model of small-company growth, stages and crises.*

### THEORY

**Three personalities in a small business (Gerber):**

- **Entrepreneur** — thinks about the future, makes changes, creates things (and some disorder).
- **Manager** — thinks about the past, turns ideas into processes, cleans up the disorder.
- **Technician** — lives in the present, focuses on *how to do the work*.
- A typical small entrepreneur (owner-manager) is roughly **10% entrepreneur, 20% manager, 70% technician**.

**Owner/manager model:**

- **Advantages:** freedom, low bureaucracy, high flexibility, innovative approach.
- **Disadvantages:** less capital for R&D/marketing/IT, fewer connections, lower specialisation than big firms (a small firm can’t have a separate department for everything).

**Model of small-company growth (stages + the crisis that ends each):**

- **Establishment** — founders’ energy drives it; one product, limited distribution; funded from personal/family money. → **Crisis of leadership.**
- **Survival** — focus on finance and investment; competition uncertain. → **Crisis of autonomy** (overtrading).
- **Growth** — need an organizational structure; develop new products. → **Crisis of management** (strong competition, expansion).
- **Expansion** — control budgets, decentralise, delegate; need long-term financing. → **Crisis of bureaucracy** (founder grows distant).
- **Maturity** — focus on cost management and productivity; may create subsidiaries; shareholder pressure.

## Q4 — Belbin Team Roles

### THEORY

**Belbin** identified **9 team roles** based on behaviour. A team doesn’t need all nine; most people are comfortable in two or three. Knowing your roles improves teamwork and communication. The nine roles fall into three groups (each with strengths and an allowable weakness):

**Thinking roles:**

- **Plant** — creative, solves hard problems (can be forgetful/distracted).
- **Monitor Evaluator** — logical, fair judgement (can lack drive).
- **Specialist** — deep expert knowledge (single-minded, narrow focus).

**Action roles:**

- **Shaper** — drives the team forward, overcomes obstacles (can upset feelings).
- **Implementer** — turns plans into efficient action (can be inflexible).
- **Completer Finisher** — quality control and error-checking at the end (can be a perfectionist).

**Social roles:**

- **Coordinator** — focuses on objectives, delegates well (can seem manipulative).
- **Teamworker** — keeps the team together (avoids confrontation).
- **Resource Investigator** — finds ideas, contacts and opportunities (over-optimistic).

Note: the role is **Shaper**, not “Sharper.”

## Q8 — Business Model Canvas (BMC)

### THEORY

The **Business Model Canvas** is a one-page tool to design and visualise a business model. It has **9 blocks** in 4 groups:

- **Infrastructure:** key partners, key activities, key resources.
- **Offer:** value proposition.
- **Customers:** customer segments, channels, customer relationships.
- **Finance:** revenue streams, cost structure.

**The 9 blocks:**

- **Customer segments** — which customer group(s) you serve.
- **Value proposition** — the product/service value that solves a problem or fills a need (newness, customisation, cost/risk reduction, accessibility).
- **Channels** — how you communicate, distribute and give post-sale support.
- **Customer relationships** — how you build and keep relationships (personal assistance, self-service, communities).
- **Revenue streams** — how you earn money (one-off or recurring; sale, leasing, licensing, usage fees); pricing can be fixed or dynamic.
- **Key resources** — the most important assets (physical, intellectual, human, financial).
- **Key activities** — what must be done to make the model work (production, problem-solving, platform/network).
- **Key partnerships** — important partners (for economies of scale, reduced risk, access to resources).
- **Cost structure** — fixed and variable costs; **cost-driven** (cut costs) vs. **value-driven** (focus on value).

## Q12 — Design Thinking

### THEORY

**Design thinking** is an iterative, **human-centred** approach used to understand users, challenge assumptions, and create innovative solutions. The aim is to turn ideas into testable prototypes quickly and test them on real customers.

## Q13 — Business Corporations & Trade License (Czech Law)

*Types, establishment, registered capital, contributions, liability, bodies, non-compete; establishment/dissolution/liquidation/insolvency. Trade license.*

### THEORY

**Business corporations = commercial companies + cooperatives.**

- **Commercial companies:**
- **Partnerships** — *unlimited (v.o.s.)* and *limited (k.s.)*.
- **Capital companies** — *limited-liability company (s.r.o.)* and *joint-stock company (a.s.)*.
- **Cooperatives** — *cooperative (družstvo)*, European Cooperative Society.

Companies are created by a **memorandum/articles of association** and **come into existence on registration in the public (commercial) register**.

**Capital companies:**

- **Limited-liability company (s.r.o.)** — the most common in CZ. Owners (members) hold a **share** proportional to their contribution. Each monetary contribution must be **paid up at least 30%** before registration. Members are liable for company debts **only up to their unpaid contribution** (limited liability). Bodies: the **general meeting** (supreme body; notice ≥15 days in advance), the **executive(s)** (statutory body, in the register), and an optional **supervisory board**.
- **Joint-stock company (a.s.)** — a “true” capital company; **minimum registered capital CZK 2,000,000**. Capital is divided into **shares** (registered or bearer; ordinary or preference). Governance can be **dualistic** (board of directors + supervisory board) or **monistic** (statutory director + administrative board).

**Partnerships:**

- **Unlimited partnership (v.o.s.)** — at least 2 persons; **jointly and severally liable with all their property**; no required registered capital; profit/loss usually split equally.
- **Limited partnership (k.s.)** — at least one **limited partner** (liable only up to unpaid contribution) and one **general partner** (liable with all property; forms the statutory body).

**Cooperative (družstvo):**

- A community of persons for the support/business of its members; **at least 3 members**; the name must include **“družstvo.”**
- Governed by **articles of association**; must keep a register of members; each member has **one cooperative share**; the contribution obligation is the **same for all** and **cannot exceed 3× the basic membership contribution**.
- **Bodies:** members’ meeting (supreme), board of directors (statutory), audit committee (supervisory). A cooperative with **fewer than 50 members** need not establish a board of directors or audit committee — the **president** acts as the statutory body.

**Lifecycle:** establishment (memorandum) → **incorporation** (registration) → operation → **dissolution** → **liquidation** (winding up assets and debts) or **insolvency** (when liabilities exceed the ability to pay).

**Trade license (živnostenský zákon):** A **trade** is a systematic activity carried out **independently, in one’s own name, on one’s own responsibility, for profit**. Some activities are *not* trades (doctors, auditors, banking, agriculture). Types:

- **Notifiable trades** (start once conditions are met): **craft (řemeslná)**, **qualified (vázaná)**, **unqualified/free (volná)**.
- **Licensed trades (koncesovaná)** — need a granted concession and proven professional competence (e.g. spirits production, road transport, travel agency).
- A **responsible representative** can be appointed; the **Trade Licensing Office** issues the authorisation and ID number; the **Trade Register (rzp.cz)** is public.

## Q14 — Accounting in the Czech Republic

### THEORY

Accounting’s purpose is to record economic reality and set the **tax liability** correctly. It’s regulated in a hierarchy:

- **Accounting Act** (general).
- **Decrees** (specify the Act).
- **Czech Accounting Standards** (practical steps). *(If they conflict, the higher level wins.)*

**Forms of accounting:**

- **Double-entry** (podvojné) — one transaction recorded in two accounts (full or simplified extent).
- **Single-entry** (jednoduché) — one transaction = one entry.

**Basic principles:** accrual principle, the accounting-entity concept, periodicity, the **matching principle** (match expenses to the revenues they relate to), one accounting per entity, and adequate disclosure.

**Classification of accounting entities** by size — **micro, small, medium, large** — judged on **three criteria**: net (total) assets, net annual turnover, and average number of employees. An entity falls into a size if it meets **at least two** of the limits for that size. **The bigger the entity, the more duties** (audit, fuller statements, more disclosure to the commercial register). A *public-interest entity* (banks, insurers, listed companies) counts as **large**.

⚠️ The exact CZK thresholds were **raised from 2024** — confirm the current figures from your course; the *principle* (three criteria, “meet at least two”) is what matters most.

**Annual financial statements:** **balance sheet** (Assets = Liabilities + Equity), **profit & loss statement** (by nature or by purpose), **cash-flow statement** (direct or indirect method), a **statement of changes in equity**, and **notes** (příloha). Larger entities must publish a fuller version to the commercial register.

**Basic transaction types and their effect:** (1) Assets ↑ & Equity/Liabilities ↑; (2) Assets ↑ & Assets ↓; (3) Liabilities/Equity ↑ & Liabilities/Equity ↓; (4) Assets ↓ & Liabilities/Equity ↓ — the balance sheet always stays balanced.

### PRACTICAL APPLICATION

- “Knowing the **size classification** matters to me practically: as my businesses grow, the accounting and audit duties grow with them, so I plan for that.”

## Q15 — Tax System in the Czech Republic

### THEORY

A **tax** is a compulsory, legally-set, non-returnable, **non-equivalent** payment to a public budget (you don’t get a direct service in return for the exact amount).

**Functions of taxes:** fiscal (fund the budget), allocating, redistributive, stimulating (e.g. tax holidays), and stabilising (progressive taxation cools an overheating economy).

**Construction (structural) elements of a tax:** tax subject (*who pays*), tax object (*what is taxed*), exemptions, **tax base**, tax period, deductions, **tax rate**, and tax reliefs.

**The Czech tax system:**

- **Direct taxes** — *income taxes* (personal and corporate) and *property taxes* (real-estate tax, road tax).
- **Indirect taxes** — *universal* (**VAT**) and *selective/excise* (alcohol, tobacco, mineral oils; energy taxes) plus *customs duty*.

**Procedural Tax Code** sets the basic rules and the **deadlines for tax returns** (for income tax, generally **3 months** after the period, extended to **6 months** if filed by a tax advisor or if the entity is audited).

*(VAT note: VAT registration becomes mandatory once turnover exceeds CZK 2,000,000 over the relevant period; the standard VAT rate is 21%.)*

## Q16 — Direct Taxes in the Czech Republic

### THEORY

**Corporate income tax (legal entities):**

- **Rate: 21%** (raised from 19% for tax periods starting in 2024). Applies to all business profits.
- **Taxpayers:** entities **seated in CZ** are taxed on **worldwide income**; entities **not seated in CZ** are taxed only on **Czech-source income**.

- The **total tax base** is built from **five partial bases:** §6 employment, §7 self-employment, §8 capital gains, §9 rental income, §10 other income.

- **Computation:** set the tax base → deduct non-taxable parts/deductions → apply the rate → subtract **tax reliefs/credits** → tax due.

**Tax residency:** a **resident** has *unlimited* tax liability (worldwide income); a **non-resident** has *limited* liability (Czech-source only). You become a resident with a permanent home **or** a usual abode (**183+ days** in the year).

**Social and health insurance (current rates):**

- **Employee:** health **4.5%** + social **7.1%** = **11.6%**.
- **Employer:** health **9%** + social **24.8%** = **33.8%** (paid on top of gross salary).

## Q17 — Business Development Assessment: Concept & Process

*Goal and purpose, planned vs. emergent approach, the analyze–plan–execute–evaluate steps, assessment at organization level.*

### THEORY

**Business (development) assessment** is a systematic, procedural analysis that reports KPIs and feedback to show the **current reality, opportunities and gaps**. It supports strategic decisions about the future direction of the business.

**Two approaches to change:**

- **Planned approach** — rationally controlled, with timetables; follows **analyze → plan → execute → evaluate**; assumes a smooth path from the current to the desired state.

**Main steps of the business development process:**

- **Analyze** — diagnose the current situation.
- **Plan** — design the interventions and actions.
- **Execute** — put the plan into action.
- **Evaluate** — gather data, analyse results, feed back.

**Assessment at organization level:** project mobilisation (work plan, kick-off) → big-picture data collection (interview management) → targeted data collection (key issues) → analysis and identification of strategic changes → action planning.

## Q18 — Organizational Life Cycles & Normative Models

*Theories for decision-making, open systems model, issues of life-cycle models, growing pains.*

### THEORY

**Organizational life cycle** — the life of an organization from creation to termination, usually **4–5 phases**: startup → emerging (survival) → growth → maturity (stabilisation) → transformation/termination.

**Open systems model** — an organization operates **within an external environment**: it takes inputs, transforms them, and returns outputs to the environment, which feed back into how it functions.

**Issues with life-cycle models** — they assume organizations develop in a similar, plannable way, but they often underweight the **human factor**: even a well-planned organization makes mistakes through its people.

**Growing pains** (Flamholtz) — signs that the internal infrastructure hasn’t kept up with the firm’s size (measured by revenue) — an “organizational development gap.” Common signs:

- Sales grow but profits don’t.
- Plans are made but not followed up.
- “Not enough hours in the day.”
- People don’t know what others are doing, or where the firm is headed.
- Too few good managers; “I have to do it myself to get it right.”
- Meetings feel like a waste of time; some people feel insecure.
